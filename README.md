# Multispectral Sensors in Remote Sensing

This repository is a coursework / academic project that surveys the major **multispectral sensors used in Earth observation** from the earliest civilian missions up to the present day. It provides a presentation deck that walks through the evolution, types, and specifications of multispectral sensors, accompanied by a structured database of those sensors and their key parameters.

## Project Overview

Multispectral sensors are at the core of modern Earth observation, supplying the imagery used in everything from land-cover mapping and agriculture to mineral exploration, environmental monitoring, and disaster response. This project compiles, organises, and visualises information on a broad set of multispectral sensors so that readers can quickly understand:

- How multispectral remote sensing has evolved over time.
- What the major sensor families and platforms are.
- How they compare in terms of spectral, spatial, and temporal resolution.
- What kinds of applications each sensor is best suited for.

The material is intended for a general technical audience — remote-sensing students, GIS practitioners, and professionals in fields such as earth sciences, mining/geology, agriculture, and environmental studies who want a consolidated reference on multispectral sensors.

## Repository Contents

- **Multispectral Sensors in RS_choladeck.pdf** — A presentation deck (slides) prepared as part of coursework. It introduces multispectral remote sensing, walks through the **timeline and types of multispectral sensors developed to date**, summarises their specifications, and highlights typical applications.
- **Multispectral_Sensors_Database (3).xlsx** — The **Multispectral Sensors Database**: a structured Excel sheet that catalogues multispectral sensors and their key parameters (e.g., platform/satellite, operating agency, launch year, number of bands, spectral range, spatial resolution, swath width, revisit time, and typical applications).

## What the Presentation Covers

The presentation deck is organised around four broad themes:

1. **Introduction to Multispectral Remote Sensing** — Basic concepts: electromagnetic spectrum, spectral bands, passive vs. active sensing, and how multispectral imagery differs from panchromatic and hyperspectral data.
2. **Timeline of Multispectral Sensors** — A chronological overview of major multispectral missions and sensors, tracing the development of Earth observation from early civilian satellites to current high-resolution and constellation-based systems.
3. **Types of Sensors and Their Specifications** — Major sensor families and platforms grouped by use case and resolution class, along with their spectral, spatial, and temporal characteristics.
4. **Applications** — Common application areas of multispectral imagery, including land-use/land-cover mapping, vegetation and agriculture monitoring, geological and mineral exploration, water resource studies, urban planning, and disaster management.

## About the Multispectral Sensors Database

The accompanying **Multispectral Sensors Database** is intended as a quick-reference table to support the presentation. For each sensor it captures the kind of information needed to compare missions side-by-side, such as:

- Sensor / instrument name and host platform or satellite.
- Operating agency or organisation.
- Launch year and operational status.
- Number of spectral bands and their general spectral range.
- Spatial resolution (per band group where applicable).
- Swath width and revisit period.
- Primary application domains.

The database is shared in `.xlsx` format so that it can be sorted, filtered, and extended easily.

## How to Use

1. Open **Multispectral Sensors in RS_choladeck.pdf** for the conceptual overview, timeline, and applications.
2. Open the **Multispectral Sensors Database** (`Multispectral_Sensors_Database (3).xlsx`) alongside the deck to look up the detailed specifications of any sensor referenced in the slides.
3. Use the database as a starting point for selecting suitable multispectral imagery for your own projects, course assignments, or research.
